            <h1 class="story-body__h1" data-asset-uri="/news/business-32531942">How much is the sea worth?</h1> 
            <p class="date date--v1" data-seconds="1430953505"><strong>7 May 2015</strong> Last updated at 00:05 BST </p> 
            <div class="map-body display-feature-phone-only"> 
             <p>If the world's seas and oceans were a national economy, how much would they be worth?</p>
             <p>Around $24 trillion (&pound;15.7tn; 21.2tn) according to the World Wildlife Foundation (WWF) in a <a href="https://www.worldwildlife.org/publications/reviving-the-oceans-economy-the-case-for-action-2015" class="story-body__link-external">report</a> this month, making it the world's seventh largest economy.</p>
             <p>The group drew up the research with the help of Boston Consulting Group and Professor Hoegh-Guldberg of the Global Change Institute in Australia.</p>
             <p>The report aims to make policy makers and business leaders view the sea as a common investment.</p>
             <p>This technique of auditing nature in financial terms is known as &quot;natural capital&quot;. It has been used extensively in global health checks like the <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/sci/tech/4391835.stm" class="story-body__link">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> in 2005, for example.</p>
             <p>But there are questions as to how accurate such figures can be when applied to nature, and calculated on this grand scale.</p>
             <p>And is monetary value really the best way to measure nature's importance to the planet, and the environment that sustains us?</p>
             <p><i>Video journalist</i>: Dougal Shaw</p>
