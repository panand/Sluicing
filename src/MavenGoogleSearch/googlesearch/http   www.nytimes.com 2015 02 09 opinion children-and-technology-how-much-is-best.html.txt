       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="1" data-total-count="1" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-1"><strong> </strong></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="14" data-total-count="15" itemprop="articleBody"><strong>To the Editor:</strong></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="59" data-total-count="74" itemprop="articleBody">Re “<a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/01/30/opinion/can-students-have-too-much-tech.html">Can Students Have Too Much Tech?</a>” (Op-Ed, Jan. 30):</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="350" data-total-count="424" itemprop="articleBody">Kudos to Susan Pinker for her excellent critique of the false promise of tech as a panacea for education. Particularly worrisome is the enormous marketing push by ed-tech companies to get digital devices in the hands of very young children, taking time away from the kind of hands-on exploration and creative play proved to be essential for learning.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="370" data-total-count="794" itemprop="articleBody">What’s also troubling, both for early childhood settings and families at home, is the lack of evidenced-based criteria for advertising claims about the educational value of these products. The market is flooded with tech products purporting to teach infants, toddlers and preschoolers everything from counting to social skills with no research to back up these claims.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="10" data-total-count="804" itemprop="articleBody">SUSAN LINN</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="13" data-total-count="817" itemprop="articleBody">SARA ADELMANN</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="6" data-total-count="823" itemprop="articleBody">Boston</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="1" data-total-count="824" itemprop="articleBody"><em> </em></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="132" data-total-count="956" itemprop="articleBody"><em>The writers are executive director and </em><em>screen time project manager</em><em>, respectively, with the Campaign for a Commercial-Free Childhood.</em></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="14" data-total-count="970" itemprop="articleBody"><strong>To the Editor:</strong></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="227" data-total-count="1197" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-2">We are two educational equity researchers who appreciate the warnings in Susan Pinker’s article. Technology for technology’s sake in the schools is not an answer for anything and can be more of a distraction, as she argues.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="243" data-total-count="1440" itemprop="articleBody">What matters is what students are learning and doing with the technology. Teaching and learning of computer science, a subject that drives innovation across all fields, must not be confused with the pouring of more technology into the schools.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="302" data-total-count="1742" itemprop="articleBody">Computer science involves knowing how things work and are made, breaking down and solving problems, critical thinking, designing systems, contributing through making, and understanding social and ethical ramifications. This is why we are committed to making computer science accessible to all students.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="354" data-total-count="2096" itemprop="articleBody">Unfortunately, too few public schools offer real computer science. And especially in schools with high numbers of African-Americans and Latinos in underresourced areas, the students are often offered only the rudimentary computer skills without the problem-solving and creative thinking that are required for understanding and innovating with technology.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="252" data-total-count="2348" itemprop="articleBody">This increasingly important knowledge, which is driving the changes in our world and lives, must not stay with the moneyed elite and families that have the resources to give their children a leg up with expensive robotic kits and computer summer camps.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="13" data-total-count="2361" itemprop="articleBody">JANE MARGOLIS</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="12" data-total-count="2373" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-3">YASMIN KAFAI</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="11" data-total-count="2384" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-4">Los Angeles</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="1" data-total-count="2385" itemprop="articleBody"><em> </em></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="195" data-total-count="2580" itemprop="articleBody"><em>Ms. Margolis is a senior researcher at the U.C.L.A. Graduate School of Education and Information Studies. Ms. Kafai is a professor at the University of Pennsylvania Graduate School of Education. </em></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="14" data-total-count="2594" itemprop="articleBody"><strong>To the Editor:</strong></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="81" data-total-count="2675" itemprop="articleBody">Susan Pinker’s article catalogs a lot of symptoms but misdiagnoses the illness.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="128" data-total-count="2803" itemprop="articleBody">The problem isn’t too much tech. It’s too few adults in some children’s lives to guide, monitor and limit their use of it.</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="18" data-total-count="2821" itemprop="articleBody">LAURENCE STEINBERG</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="12" data-total-count="2833" itemprop="articleBody">Philadelphia</p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="1" data-total-count="2834" itemprop="articleBody"><em> </em></p>
       <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="61" data-total-count="2895" itemprop="articleBody"><em>The writer is a professor of psychology at Temple University.</em></p>
